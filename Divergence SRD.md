# The Basics

---

This section will go over the absolute basics of Divergence, which every player will need to know before even making a character. All of this will come naturally to people who have played even one TTRPG before, but for the benefit of those who haven’t, the absolute fundamentals will be laid out here. 

## Skill Checks, Attacks, and Saves

When playing Divergence, you’ll be asked to roll dice. A lot. And more often than not, it will be for either a skill check, an attack, or a save. And when making your character, you’ll be conceptualizing mechanics that will require you or other players to make such rolls. Out of a conflict, you’ll primarily be making skill checks, but during a conflict, you’ll have to worry about all three.

You have to make a skill check if your character is presented with some kind of challenge that they wish to overcome. This may be breaking down a door, or sneaking past a sentry. To make a skill check, simply roll a D20 and add any relevant modifiers to the result. If the result is equal to or greater than the Difficulty Class (DC), which will be given by the GM, then you succeed. If the result is below the DC, then you either fail, or succeed but at some cost to yourself.

In a conflict, you must make an attack if you wish to harm an opposing character. To make an attack, you must state your target, roll to hit, and then roll for damage if applicable. Rolling to hit is similar to a skill check \- roll a D20 and add the relevant modifiers and compare the results against your target’s Evasion, which functions similarly to DC. If the attack is successful, then you roll for damage. Doing this depends on the attack you’re using, but it typically involves one or more dice in addition to the relevant modifiers. A general rule of thumb to differentiate skill checks from attacks is that attacks typically can only be made during a conflict, while skill checks can be made any time.

Unlike the other two types of rolls, you never make a save of your own initiative. A save is almost always forced on you by some other character, typically with the goal of imparting some negative effect. Rolling a save is similar to rolling a skill check or an attack \- roll a D20 and add any relevant modifiers, and then compare the results against the DC, which depends on whatever is forcing you to make the save. Additionally, you will more than likely force others to make saves as well, and the DC they must roll against will vary depending on your character.

In some cases, you’ll be called to participate in a contested skill check, typically whenever two characters are attempting to outdo each other in some way, such as in a race or a game. To do so, the characters involved simply make the relevant skill check, and the one with the highest result succeeds. In the case that there is a tie, the characters may opt to roll again, or the GM may introduce some new aspect to the contest to make things more interesting. Contested skill checks typically occur outside of combat, and can be done with any skill. Additionally, contested skill checks don’t always involve the same skill. A character attempting to hide may roll one skill, and another character attempting to search for them may roll another, for example.

## Advantage & Disadvantage

For whatever reason, your character may find themself in a situation where unique circumstances have a material effect on their performance in a task, favorable or otherwise. They may have the element of surprise on their side, or they may be bogged down by mud while trying to flee from an enemy. In any case, the effect of both internal and external conditions on your character’s abilities is generally represented by advantage and disadvantage. 

When making a roll, you may be asked to roll it with \+1 Advantage, most likely due to the favorable conditions your character finds themself in. To do so, make your roll as normal, adding all the relevant modifiers, but roll the D20 with a D6 equal to how much Advantage the roll has. The highest roll will be the one factored into the final result. For example, the result of a check with \+3 Advantage will be the highest roll among the 3 D6s coupled with the D20, accounting for modifiers. If asked to make a roll with Disadvantage, do the same, but the lowest roll will be the one factored into the result. Advantages and disadvantages cancel each other out.

Advantages and Disadvantages with damage die work differently, a \+1 or \-1 disadvantage shall prompt the player to roll their damage roll twice. If they have advantage, they may take the higher result, and if they have disadvantage, they must take the lower result.

### Critical Hits

Critical Hits give the character who achieved it a \+1 advantage to their damage die. They occur when a character’s attack roll results in a 20 and above.

## Fate Points

Fate Points (FP) are a resource available to all player characters which enables them to influence the outcome of their actions and use their most powerful Abilities. All characters start with a maximum of 3 FP, which they may increase as they gain Milestones. FP is recovered whenever a character takes a Full Restore. Additionally, the GM may decide to award players FP when they perform an amazing feat in battle, play their character remarkably well, or do anything else of merit that’s deemed worthy of reward.

A character’s Fatebreaker, in addition to their Overcharged Abilities, costs FP to use. FP can also be used to force a success on any failed roll, at a cost of 1 FP per skill check, attack roll, or save.

## Milestones

After completing a storyline that progresses the story in a significant way, your GM may choose to reward the players with a Milestone, giving the players a chance to improve their character’s Attributes, Skills, and Abilities. Upon reaching a Milestone, you must increase one of your character’s Attributes by 1, and increase one of your character’s Skills by 2\. Every two Milestones, you must choose between giving your character an additional Ability Slot or raise their maximum Fate Points by 1\. 

New players joining a party are suggested to follow the current party’s milestone level. They may choose to opt out of this standard if their suggestion is within reason.

### Milestone Bonus

A Milestone Bonus is a bonus added to all saves and attack rolls a character makes, and determines their Save DC. A character’s Milestone Bonus is equal to the number of Milestones divided by two, rounded down. 

For example, if a character with 4 Milestones and a Power Attribute of 3 wants to make a Basic Attack with Power, they must roll a d20, and add their Power Attribute (3), and their Milestone Bonus (2) to the roll.

# Character Creation

---

Character creation in Divergence is similar, but at the same time very different from other RPGs. Initially, you will allocate Attribute points and choose proficiencies as normal, but as for what makes your character *mechanically unique,* that will all come from you. There is no compendium for you to make character builds with. Rather, you build your character from the ground up, using what is provided to you here as a framework.

All necessary information regarding your character, which includes their attributes, abilities, physical description, and backstory, among other things, is recorded on a character sheet. The character sheet will serve as your reference for everything your character is capable of, and it will also be how you keep track of their condition and wellbeing. Your GM should always have access to your character sheet, and while you don’t necessarily have to share it with other players, it’s highly encouraged for you to do so as it may help them craft their own characters. This section will go over all the different parts of the character sheet and how they play into your character’s narrative and mechanical identity. The Divergence character sheet will be provided alongside this document. 

*To illustrate the character creation process, each of the following subsections will contain an example of how someone may fill in each respective part of the character sheet. These examples will be enclosed in block quotes such as this one.* 

## Profile

This is the first part of your character sheet, and narratively speaking, the most important. As a general rule of thumb, you should always start with the Profile, because your character’s identity as an individual may trickle down to how you fill in the subsequent parts of the character sheet. Here, you’ll only find two fields, one for your character’s portrait, and another for a general description for your character. You should put a lot of thought into your character’s description, and do everything in your power to make sure that it succinctly and accurately captures the narrative essence of your character. 

Think of your character’s description here as their *narrative thesis,* in that someone should be able to, at a glance, glean your character’s defining traits from it. A character’s description bears no mechanical consequence on the character, but it’s important in that it will help inform how the character acts as a person.

*Louise wants to create a character born to a noble family of magic users. She wants to emphasize how his family’s expectations shaped him as a person, and how that bleeds over to his disposition. So, she writes the following description:* 

*“As the eldest son of House Vasiliev, Purga bears the weight of his family's expectations on his shoulders. He is under constant scrutiny by his mother, who expects him to carry on the family's traditions with honor and excellence. Because of this, he has grown to be arrogant and neurotic.”*

## Core Ability

After finishing the Profile, you should move on to your character’s Core Ability. In essence, your character’s Core Ability is the defining factor that sets them apart *mechanically.* It contains both mechanical and narrative elements, but primarily, your character’s Core Ability comes into play during combat, where most of the game’s hard rules come into play. To put it succinctly, if your character’s Profile contains your character’s *narrative* thesis, their Core Ability contains their *mechanical* thesis, which becomes relevant mostly during a conflict.

A character’s Core ability consists of three components: Innate, Basic Attack, and Fatebreaker. 

### Innate

Your character’s Innate is the first component of their Core Ability, which itself contains two extremely important parts of your character sheet: a description of your character’s abilities, and the mechanical *core* that will dictate how your character will be played. That sounds quite vague now, but it will be explained in detail in this subsection.

To create your character’s Innate, start by describing in detail what it is your character is capable of in a conflict. Ask yourself: *given ideal circumstances, how would my character fight someone?* Do they use martial arts? If so, what kind? Maybe they use firearms? Do they prefer rifles, handguns, or a mix of both? Do they have some kind of superpower? Do they cast magical spells? What kind of spells do they cast, and how?

However, this is not meant to be a comprehensive list of *all* of your character’s combat capabilities. As much as possible, focus on what makes your character narratively and functionally unique in a fight. If your character’s a gunslinger, go into detail about their unique and special way of fighting with firearms. Even if your character knows hand-to-hand combat on the side, that doesn’t warrant anything more than a passing mention. Focus on what makes them *unique.*

You, your GM, and maybe even other players, will be referencing this part of your character’s Core Ability frequently, as it defines the scope of what your character is mechanically capable of in a conflict. And, most importantly, it will be the basis of your character’s Abilities. 

*Louise envisions Purga slinging jagged icicles and conjuring ferocious blizzards in battle with magic, sending all his foes to an icy grave. So when she begins making Purga’s Core Ability, she starts by clearly stating the extent of his combat abilities, making sure to emphasize his unique magical power to command temperature and wind at will.*

*“Purga's Grimoire, Тундра, grants him the fearsome ability to bring the cruel ferocity of the Siberian tundra to any battlefield. It allows him to rapidly lower the temperature of the surrounding environment, to the extent that he can instantaneously freeze the moisture in the air to create snow and icicles. Additionally, he can create gusts of wind at will, which he uses to propel his attacks.”*

If you’re satisfied with this, then you may move onto filling in the other parts of the character sheet. However, you also have the option of giving your character more mechanical depth and identity by creating a mechanical representation of their Innate. This resembles a Slotted Ability, which will be elaborated on later, but as it’s a part of their Core Ability, it is inextricable from the character both as an individual and as a functional in-game entity. Thus, unlike a Slotted Ability, your character will always have access to it. If you previously described that your character prefers to strike from the shadows and is an expert at staying hidden, then maybe they have the option of becoming Hidden anywhere, and not only when completely out of sight like other characters. Or if you described your character to be a crack shot with a rifle, then maybe they have some kind of ability to mark their targets, giving them some kind of advantage in a conflict. This way, your character’s Innate goes beyond just being a description of what your character can do in a fight, but also a tangible and material mechanic in itself that solidifies your character’s style of play. 

However, since your character has access to their Innate at all times, take care as to not make it too powerful. Instead of approaching your character’s Innate as a way for them to deal damage directly, think of it as a sort of framework that supports their other abilities, and incentivizes a certain style of play. Additionally, ensure that your character’s Innate is clear and focused; don’t try to cram several mechanics and bonuses into it, as that can be both unbalanced and difficult to manage. Returning to the marksman example from earlier: maybe marking targets doesn’t do anything on its own, but the character’s other Slotted Abilities can only work on targets that have been marked. Thus, the player is incentivized to hang back and spend time choosing their targets carefully, before letting loose and dealing damage.

But ultimately, what your character’s Innate is is entirely up to you and your GM. It can be as simple as a bonus in certain Skills, or as complex as an entire compendium of beasts that the character may summon to fight for them. As long as everyone around the table agrees that it’s fair and balanced, then go ahead and let your imagination run wild.

*Louise doesn’t want Purga to be a damage-focused character since that role has already been fulfilled by another player. So, she instead decides to build Purga around tanking and area control. She continues writing Purga’s Innate, eventually coming up with a mechanic that lets Purga project an aura of cold around him. This aura isn’t exceptionally powerful by itself, but Louise plans to create Abilities for Purga that allow him to afflict targets within the aura with status conditions and hinder their movement.*

### Basic Attack

Your character’s basic attack is their simplest form of defending themselves in a conflict. When all other abilities fail, your character will *always* be able to fall back on their Basic Attack. And as the name implies, your character’s Basic Attack is extremely straightforward, but as with every other part of your character’s Core Ability, you get to put your own spin on it. 

To create your character’s Basic Attack, first determine if it is ranged or melee. Then, decide if it will use your character’s Martial or Power Attribute. Finally, feel free to flavor it however you wish. Maybe it’s a simple left hook, or a fire bolt, or a single round fired off from your character’s signature sidearm.

| Basic Attack (Melee) |
| :---- |
| *Melee, Variable, Basic*  **1 AP, 0 END 1d6 \+ MAR/POW** Any subsequent Basic Attacks made after the first one on your turn cost 2 AP and do not benefit from modifiers.  *Ol’ reliable.* |

| Basic Attack (Ranged) |
| :---- |
| *Ranged, Variable, Basic*  **1 AP, 0 END Range: 8  1d6 \+ MAR/POW** Any subsequent Basic Attacks made after the first one on your turn cost 2 AP and do not benefit from modifiers.  *Ol’ reliable.* |

*Louise imagines that Purga may have inherited some kind of ceremonial sword that he carries with him at all times, as a reminder of his duty to uphold his family’s legacy. In a pinch, she thinks Purga may draw it to fight in close-quarters combat. So, she gives Purga a melee Basic Attack based on his Martial Attribute.*

### Fatebreaker

Now that you’ve described the general extent of what makes your character uniquely powerful, now it’s time for you to imagine what that power may look like at its absolute peak. When all cards are down and it’s make or break, *this* is what your character brings to the table. This is your character’s  Bankai, their Ultra Instinct, their Domain Expansion. This is their *Fatebreaker,* the peak of your character’s abilities. 

Since you’ve already defined what your character’s combat capabilities are in the Innate component of the Core Ability, the heavy lifting is mostly over and done with. From there, you can take one of two approaches with creating your character’s Fatebreaker. You may create what is functionally the natural evolution of their Innate, which is often the approach taken if the character’s Innate is packed with mechanics that the character is meant to play around. On the other hand, you may choose to create a Fatebreaker that’s entirely unique \- something almost completely out of left field. Both approaches are valid. As long as it falls within the scope of the character’s Innate, then it can be their Fatebreaker. Either way, a Fatebreaker is often impressive, impactful, and most importantly of all, *cool.*

*Louise comes up with a couple of ideas for Purga’s Fatebreaker. Since she already made an Innate that gives him an aura of cold, which many of his abilities are based around, she considers making his Fatebreaker a powerful upgrade for that aura. Maybe its area of effect can increase, and enemies who stay inside it for too long start taking damage. She also considers making his Fatebreaker a huge, one time attack where he summons a massive iceberg which he hurls at his enemies.*

Because of their tide-turning nature, Fatebreakers always cost 2 Fate Points, 4 END and around 1 AP to use, no matter what. A Fatebreaker cannot be used if the character it belongs to has insufficient Fate points. Naturally, this makes Fatebreakers fairly costly, so cooperate with your GM to make sure that they’re effective enough to make a splash in combat, without massively undermining the balance of the game.

The process of creating a Fatebreaker is very similar to creating a Slotted Ability, which is explained in detail later on in the section on Character Creation. If you’re following along with this document while making your character sheet, then come up with a general idea for your character’s Fatebreaker for now, and wait until you’ve finished reading the subsection on Ability Creation before proceeding with how it will be represented with game mechanics.

## Attributes

With your character’s Core Ability fully fleshed out, you should have a fairly good idea of how your character will play: if they prefer using weapons or magic, if they’re particularly durable or nimble, and so on. Your character’s Attributes represent their most important proficiencies and attributes, which they lean on in difficult situations. They’ll help you further define how your character is meant to be played, and will heavily influence how they perform in combat.

Your character has five Attributes: Martial, Power, Agility, Vitality, and Grit (shortened to MAR, POW, AGI, VIT, and GRT). Each of the five Attributes perform different functions in battle, and also confer unique advantages to your character should you choose to invest into them. When first making your character, you’re given an array of values: 3, 2, 1, 0, and \-1. You must assign which of your character’s Attributes start with each of these values. At each Milestone, you may increase any of your character’s Attributes by 1, to a maximum of 8\.

*Since Louise knows that Purga will be relying on his magic spells a lot, she decides to make Power his highest Attribute at 3\. And since he’ll often be up close and personal with his foes, and thus taking more than his fair share of hits, she decides to make Vitality his second highest Attribute at 2\. He also wields a sword, but he won’t be using it often so she sets his Martial to 1\. Lastly, she sets his Agility to 0 and Grit to \-1, leaving Purga with a Attribute spread of 3 Power, 2 Vitality, 1 Martial, 0 Agility, and \-1 Grit.*

### Martial

Your character’s Martial Attribute represents their proficiency in both armed and unarmed combat. This is how skilled your character is in a fight, barring any supernatural abilities they may have. Characters who specialize in martial arts, sword fighting, gunslinging, and anything of the like rely on their Martial. Any attack  that is made bare-handed, with a weapon, or with any other non-supernatural means, receives a bonus to its respective attack roll  equal to the character’s Martial.

### Power

Your character’s Power Attribute represents the potency of their supernatural powers, whatever they may be. Power is a catch-all Attribute that can help mechanically represent the esoteric and fantastical components of your character’s Core Ability, whether it’s their repertoire of spells, psychic superpowers, gamma radiation induced mutations, or anything in between. If it falls beyond what is typically covered by Martial, then it can be safely assumed that it falls under the Power Attribute. Any attack that is made through supernatural means receives a bonus to its respective attack roll equal to the character’s Power.

### Agility

Your character’s Agility Attribute represents their overall dexterity and reaction speed under pressure. Very agile characters have an easier time moving around the battlefield and avoiding enemy attacks. All characters receive a bonus to their base Evasion of 10 equal to their Agility Attribute, to a maximum of \+10, an additional 1 Movement per two points of Agility, to a maximum of \+3, and a bonus equal to their Agility Attribute to Move Quickly skill rolls.

### Vitality

Your character’s Vitality Attribute represents their physical hardiness and ability to resist all manner of ailments. Vitality dictates how much damage your character can take before taking a Mortal Wound, and how well they’ll be able to resist whatever physical negative status effects their enemies attempt to inflict on them. All characters receive a bonus to their base HP of 20 equal to their Vitality Attribute multiplied by 5\. Along with that, every 2 points invested into Vitality equates into 1 point of Armor for that character. A point of Armor is another layer of protection for the character, providing a d6 of reduction to every instance of damage that character takes. That means that a character with 2 Armor could possibly reduce up to 12 damage against non-armor piercing attacks.

### Grit

Your character’s Grit Attribute represents their mental fortitude and willingness to endure in the face of overwhelming odds. Grit will determine how often your character can use their Abilities, as well as how well they can resist attacks aimed at their mind. Characters regain an additional point of Endurance at the end of their turn, this can be increased by another 1 per two points of Grit. So, characters with \-1, 0, and 1 Grit will gain 1 Endurance every end of their turn. While characters with 2, 4, and 6 Grit will gain 2\. 3, and 4 Endurance at the end of their turn respectively.

## SSkills

Your character is much more than what they’re capable of in battle. The life they led prior to embarking on their campaign, whatever that may have been, has given them the opportunity to pick up a few unique skills that can, and will, prove useful in their current adventure. For instance, a former thief might be handy with a lockpick, and an engineer more than likely knows their way around a toolbox. Your character’s Skills represent the various proficiencies they’ve picked up over the years, and the ones they pick up over the course of the campaign. While not typically used in combat, Skills find their use in narrative play, where your character may be faced with tricky puzzles and social situations. 

A character starts out with 3 points to allocate to the skills they are proficient at. Each point adds a \+2 to the roll of that chosen skill whenever it is used in a situation. Every time a character reaches a new Milestone they gain another point to allocate to one of the skills, or upgrade one of the skills they already have an existing point on to add another \+2 to bonus to that skill’s roll. So two points would grant a \+4, three points would grant a \+6, and so on. ## Slotted Abilities

Your character’s Slotted Abilities are the most mechanically impactful part of the character sheet, second only to their Core Ability. They represent the various applications of your character’s Core Ability, showing all the clever and unique ways they use their gifts. A Slotted Ability can be a specific martial arts technique, like a low kick your character has practiced a thousand times, or it can be a spell, like an inferno that your character can conjure at will. Anything that falls under the scope of your character’s Core Ability can be refined to work as a Slotted Ability, so long as it’s reasonable in terms of power. If it had to be described in video game terms: your character’s Fatebreaker is their ultimate,  while their Slotted Abilities are their Q and E abilities. Still impactful, but smaller in scale and considerably more disposable. 

Slotted Abilities aren’t limited only to attacks. Depending on your character’s Core Ability, Slotted Abilities can give your character the capability to inflict status conditions, manipulate the surrounding environment, heal their allies, and so much more. If you take the time to come up with novel ways your character could apply their unique skills, then their identity will really start to shine through in their Slotted Abilities. 

As with the Core Ability, you will have to create your character’s Slotted Abilities from scratch. When doing so, try to keep balance in mind, and as always, consult with your GM about anything you’re unsure about. Upon creating a Slotted Ability for your character, make sure to have your GM approve it before using it in-game. More often than not, Slotted Abilities need at minimum a few tweaks before being allowed on the character sheet.

There are a few key things that are highly recommended you keep in mind when making a Slotted Ability for your character. Firstly, consider how *fun* this Ability will be, not just for you, but also for people around the table. Consider how it ties into your character’s personality, and try to imagine the flourishes they may add to it when using said Ability in battle. Maybe they fire off a silver bullet blessed by the Vatican, or maybe they throw a certain punch passed onto them by their late master. It could be as simple as a catchphrase your character throws out whenever they use the Ability. There’s much more to an Ability than the damage it deals \- they’re a way for you to distinguish your character even further, and make them more than a set of values on a character sheet.

Additionally, you should also consider how the Ability interacts with the environment and other characters. Abilities that deal straight damage are valid, yes, but they can be so much more. You can have an Ability inflict a status condition unique to your character, or fire off an attack with a wide area of effect, or somehow alter the surrounding area to the character’s advantage. If you have a fun concept in mind for your character’s Ability, make sure to back it up with the appropriate mechanics to ensure that the Ability is represented properly in-game. Likewise, if you’ve thought of an interesting in-game interaction that you want to turn into an Ability for your character, remember to supplement it with the right amount of flavor that ties it back to your character as an individual.

Lastly, there’s also the consideration of balance. As a general rule of thumb, stronger Slotted Abilities should have a suitably higher cost. Recall that your character’s Fatebreaker always costs one Fate Point to use. This is because it’s your character’s most powerful move, and thus it consumes the most valuable resource in the game. Slotted Abilities should follow the same principle, although with resources that are not as scarce as to make sure that your character can still utilize them on a relatively frequent basis. Most of the time, Abilities consume Endurance, which always resets at the end of a conflict and can be replenished if the character chooses to Recover. More powerful Abilities should consume more Endurance. If your character has other resources from their Core Ability, then the same applies to them. 

### Minor Abilities

When creating a Slotted Ability for your character, you may choose to give it the Minor trait. Minor Slotted Abilities are Abilities that are relatively weaker compared to your character’s other Abilities, and therefore shouldn’t take up an entire Ability Slot all by themselves. Minor Abilities make less of a splash in combat compared to other Abilities, but consume significantly less resources. Like regular Slotted Abilities, Minor Abilities can do a wide range of things limited only by your character’s Core Ability, but on a far narrower scale. 

For example, a Slotted Ability that only gives a passive bonus to a character’s Deceive Skill in a certain situation should be a Minor Ability. An attack that only deals 1D6 \+ POW damage in a small Area of Effect should be a Minor Ability as well. Ultimately, it’s up to your GM to finalize a Slotted Ability’s status as a Minor Ability.

### Ability Slots

Ability Slots dictate how many Slotted Abilities your character can have active at a time. An Ability slot can hold either one ordinary Slotted Ability, or two Minor ones. All characters start with three Ability Slots, and may choose to gain another Slot every two Milestones. You can choose which of your character’s Slotted Abilities are active and are taking up an Ability Slot during the Preparation phase of any conflict. All Slotted Abilities not currently active and taking up an Ability Slot are stored in a character’s Ability Pool, and can be swapped with an active Ability during Preparation.

## Ability Creation

Creating your character’s Fatebreaker and Slotted Abilities can be fairly overwhelming, given that you’re given what is effectively a blank slate to work on. However, there are a few guidelines that can help keep you on the right track while creating your character's abilities. Some of these guidelines are mere suggestions, while others are hard rules that all players must follow. When in doubt, consult your GM about anything you may be unsure about. They’ll help you balance your character’s abilities to make sure that they’re capable, but not overpowered at the same time.

### D6 of Effectiveness

While D20s are always used to determine successes for attack rolls, skill checks, and saves, D6s are always used to determine the impact of any given Ability, whether it’s a Slotted Ability or a Fatebreaker. All Abilities are scaled starting with the number of D6s being rolled to determine their effectiveness. By counting the number of D6s involved in resolving an Ability’s effects, we can gauge roughly how powerful an Ability is in-game. The more D6s, the more powerful.

Recall that a character’s Basic Attack always deals 1D6 \+ MAR/POW damage. In the vast majority of cases, a character’s Basic Attack will always have the same damage formula, regardless of how many Milestones they have. When first making Abilities for your character, their Basic Attack can be a good point of comparison for determining the strength of an Ability. For example, an Ability that does 2D6 damage is relatively weak, considering that a Basic Attack costs no Endurance, and will only cost 1 AP. On the other hand, an attack that deals 4D6 damage will, on average, deal over twice the damage a max roll Basic Attack is capable of dealing. From this metric alone, you can see that the second Ability is significantly more powerful, and that will help you determine if it’s balanced relative to the other players and the state of the campaign. 

There are no hard rules that say how many D6s of effectiveness an Ability can have at any given Milestone. It falls on you, the player, to use the freedom you’re given responsibly. Balance is a delicate thing when all the players are given free reign over what their characters can do, so everyone should do their part to help maintain the balance of the game by being conservative with the effectiveness of their Abilities. If you’re unsure about how many D6s of effectiveness your character’s Ability should have, consult with your GM. They’ll have a full picture of what state the campaign is in, and can help you make balanced Abilities.

### Ability Traits

Beyond dealing damage, an Ability can have an effectively endless range of effects depending on the character it belongs to. Ability Traits classify Abilities based on their effects, which helps both the player and the GM determine what an Ability does and should cost at a glance. Additionally, Traits help form a loose web of association between Abilities that belong to different characters. For instance, an Ability with the *Armor Piercing* Trait should probably ignore the effects of an Ability with the *Armor* Trait. Of course, there’s context that Traits cannot possibly account for to consider, but in most cases they help determine how interactions between Abilities should be resolved.

Players shouldn’t concern themselves with giving their characters’ Abilities extremely granular Traits. An Ability that has a character swinging their sword a certain way doesn’t need a *Sword* or *Steel* Trait, for example. A *Slashing* Damage Type Trait will do. Use general and abstractional wording when choosing Traits for your Abilities as much as possible, so that they come into play more often and can interact with other Abilities more effectively.

Below are some of the most common Traits you may want to give to an Ability for your character. This is by no means exhaustive, but it should cover most Abilities. If there’s a Trait that you’d like to give to one of your character’s Abilities that isn’t on this list, consult your GM and figure out how to implement it with their help.

#### Activation

An Ability’s Activation Trait can either be *Action, Passive,* or *Reaction.* An Ability that is activated on the turn of the character with the Ability has the *Action* Trait. An Ability that is always active regardless of a character’s actions has the *Passive* Trait. And lastly, an Ability that can be activated on another character’s turn given that prior conditions are met has the *Reaction* Trait. All Abilities have an Activation Trait. 

#### Range

All Abilities have a limited effective range that must be specified with its Range Trait. An Ability’s Range Trait can either be *Melee* or *Ranged*. Abilities with the *Melee* Trait  can only affect targets immediately adjacent to the character with the Ability. On the other hand, an Ability with the *Ranged* Trait can affect targets up to a specified number of tiles away from the acting character. This number is written next to the Trait. For example, an Ability that lets a character heal someone up to 10 tiles away has the *Range (10)* Trait.

This Trait is often combined with the Area of Effect Trait (AoE) to represent Abilities that have an AoE within a certain range. For example, an Ability with the *Ranged (10)* and *Blast* *(3)* Traits can affect a 3x3 area anywhere within 10 tiles of the acting character.

#### Area of Effect

An Ability that affects multiple characters within a predefined area has one of the Area of Effect (AoE) Traits. Depending on the nature of the Ability, its AoE Trait can either be *Aura, Blast, Cone, or Line,* which corresponds to the shape of the Ability’s AoE when used. An Ability that has a uniquely shaped AoE should have a unique Trait to denote this. Additionally, when writing an Ability’s Area of Effect Trait, make sure to include the radius of the Ability’s AoE in tiles. For example, an Ability that creates a wall that’s represented as a 5 tile long line on the map should have the *Line (5)* Trait.

###### Line

Line based abilities cover an Area of a vertical, horizontal, or diagonal line, they cover a number of tiles equal to the number they’re assigned to. Line abilities act differently depending if it is a *Melee* or *Ranged* attack. A *Melee* Line ability would have the Line start at the character’s adjacent tiles and extend outwards to any direction they wish. A *Ranged* Line ability can appear anywhere **within** the ability’s specified *Range* value. So a *Ranged (8) Line (8)* ability cannot have the Line start at the edge of the Range and protrude outwards for an effective Range of 16 Tiles.

###### Blast

Blast based abilities cover an Area of a square. The number attached next to the Blast indicates the number that is squared, therefore a *Blast (3)* ability would cover a 3x3 area and a *Blast (6)* would cover 6x6.

###### Cone

Cone based abilities cover an Area of a triangle, the effective Range of which is specified in Owlbear. Cone abilities act differently depending if it is a *Melee* or *Ranged* attack. A *Melee* Cone ability would have the Cone’s triangle tip start at the character’s adjacent tiles and extend outwards to any direction they wish. A *Ranged* Cone ability can appear anywhere **within** the ability’s specified *Range* value. So a *Ranged (8) Cone (5)* ability cannot have the Cone start at the edge of the Range and protrude outwards for an effective Range of 13 Tiles.

###### Aura

Aura based abilities cover an Area of a square around the character The number assigned to the Aura ability is equal to how many tiles the Aura extends from the character. So an *Aura (2)* ability will extend 2 tiles from the character and thus cover a 5x5 area with the character in its center.

#### Type

An Ability that deals damage should have a Type Trait to indicate how exactly the Ability harms its targets. This is important because some Abilities may have special interactions with other Abilities that deal damage of a certain Type. There is no exhaustive list of Types an Ability can have, as it falls on the player to create the appropriate Type Trait for their character’s Ability, with the help of the GM. 

As a rule of thumb, you should use general and abstracted wording when giving Abilities Type Trait to ensure that they can actually be called on in the appropriate situations. For example, the Type Trait *Punch* is too specific, even if it’s accurate. *Bludgeoning* is more likely to get called on by other Abilities as it’s applicable to more situations.

Abilities can have multiple Type Traits, although it isn’t necessary. However, at minimum, a damaging Ability should have either the *Physical, Magic,* or *Mental* Type Traits to indicate what kind of attack it is in the broadest sense. Any Type Traits beyond that are optional, but encouraged.

#### Special Property

An ability that creates some kind of special effect on the field needs to have a Special Property Trait to indicate what kind of Ability it is. For example, an Ability that lets a character raise a shield needs to have the *Shield* Trait, an Ability that creates difficult terrain should have the *Terrain* Trait, and so on. Your GM can look over an Ability’s Traits to make sure that they line up with the other Abilities in the campaign. As is the case with Type Traits, make sure to use general wording that isn’t too specific to the Ability.

#### Status 

An Ability that inflicts a status condition should have the *Status* Trait. For instance, an Ability that inflicts the Freezing condition must have the *Status* Trait to indicate that.

##### Status Conditions

Status Conditions are effects that can be inflicted upon your character by a variety of means, mostly through the Abilities of other characters. These can be either positive or negative. Negative Status Conditions can be called Debuffs, while positive Status Conditions can be called Buffs. Debuffs are typically applied by forcing the target to make a save, the Condition being applied on a failure. Either way, Status Conditions typically have a limited duration, and in the case of Debuffs, can be removed with a save. 

Characters can have multiple Status Conditions at a time, but in the case that a character receives another instance of a Status Condition they already have from another source, the stronger instance takes precedence if applicable. For instance, if a character has a Status Condition with 1 of 3 rounds of duration remaining, and they receive the Status Condition again, the duration is reset to the maximum. In any other case where the Status Conditions are identical, the new instance is ignored.

A Status Condition can be classified as Quick, Persistent, Countdown, Permanent, or Conditional, which are indicated along the *Status* Trait in the Status’ respective Ability as a Sub-Trait. For example, a Quick Status Condition has the *Status (Quick)* Trait. A Condition isn’t necessarily tied to a single duration, and can have different durations depending on the Ability applying them. The Vulnerable Condition, for example, is typically classified as Quick, but in some cases can be Permanent, such as if being applied by a Fatebreaker.

###### *Quick* 

A Quick Status Condition only lasts until the end of the affected character’s next turn. Quick Status Conditions are typically very impactful, and thus must last only a short while. For example, the Blinded Condition limits the affected character’s line of sight to adjacent tiles. This Condition is very powerful, and so it is classified as Quick. Similarly powerful conditions should always be Quick.

###### *Persistent*

A Persistent Status Condition lasts until the affected character succeeds on a save made at the end of their turn, typically the same one that they had to make when first receiving the Status Condition.  A Persistent Status Condition typically has some recurring effect on the target at the end of each of their turns, until the Condition wears off with a successful save. In the case of a Persistent Condition that causes the affected character to take damage, they always make the save first before taking the damage. Persistent Status Conditions are typically Debuffs, as they require the affected character to make a save. For example, the Poisoned Condition causes the affected character to take 1d6+2 damage at the end of each of their turns until they succeed on the VIT save that the character rolled when first receiving it.

###### *Countdown*

A Countdown Status Condition lasts for a set number of rounds, and may have some recurring effect on the target at the end of each of their turns until the Condition wears off. On the last round of the Condition’s duration, it takes effect on the affected character before being cleared. Countdown Conditions are Buffs more often than not, but they can be Debuffs as well. For example, the Regeneration Condition restores 2d6+3 HP to the affected character at the end of each of their turns for 3 rounds. 

###### *Permanent*

A Permanent Status Condition lasts until the end of the conflict in which they were applied. By their nature, Permanent Conditions are very powerful and are thus reserved for Fatebreakers or Abilities of a similar caliber. However, they can still be cleared by other Abilities that allow Conditions to be removed. At the end of the conflict in which the Permanent Condition was applied, it’s automatically cleared and does not carry over into the next conflict. For example, the Cursed Condition prevents the affected character from regaining HP until the end of the battle.

###### *Conditional*

A Conditional Status Condition is only in effect for as long as the affected character fulfills a certain condition, specified in the affecting Ability’s description. Once the affected character no longer fulfills that condition, the Status will no longer affect them unless they begin to fulfill that condition again. 

#### Reliable 

An ability that can consistently deal a minimum amount of damage regardless of rolls should have the *Reliable* Trait. For instance, an Ability that has Reliable (4) will always do 4 damage regardless if the d20 roll is lower than the evasion. Reliable trait abilities tend to be attached to damaging Fatebreakers, or High-END abilities. Reliable abilities should usually have damage values lower than the average of what the attack would have done, and they also do not trigger status effects.

#### Multi-Hit

An ability that has multiple attacks within a single instance should have the *Multi-Hit* Trait. For instance, an Ability that has Multi-Hit(2) can roll the attacking die twice.

#### Armor-Breaking (AB)

An ability that bypasses any Armor properties should have the *Armor-Breaking* Trait. For instance, an ability with Armor-Breaking trait would deal damage that bypasses mitigating Armor roll.

#### Explosive 

An ability with the Explosive Trait will bypass the typical Evasion value, instead it will prompt all characters hit by it to make a Save, Typically the save would utilize the AGI stat, if the character succeeds they gain Resistance to that specific attack, if they fail, they take the full damage.

#### Cooldown

Cooldown indicates that an ability cannot be activated for a set amount of rounds after use. An ability with the trait *Cooldown (2)* will require 2 full rounds to pass before being able to be used again.

#### Recharge

Recharge abilities are abilities that can only be used when passing a D6 roll. For instance, an Ability that has Recharge (4) would need to roll 4 and above on the D6 to use the ability. This trait of abilities is usually reserved for Enemy characters, and is not recommended for player use.

### Save DC

A character’s Save DC determines what other characters must roll against when attempting to resist or avoid the effects of their Abilities. Your Save DC is equal to 10 \+ your Milestone Bonus. When an Ability forces another character to make a save, the DC that the affected character is rolling against is always the affecting character’s Save DC. When making Abilities for your character, avoid making individual DCs for each Ability, and instead have them all refer to your Save DC.

### Ability Cost

All Abilities should have a cost equivalent to their effectiveness. For Fatebreakers, this cost will always be 2 Fate Points and a minimum of 1 AP and 4 Endurance, but for a Slotted Ability, it’s up to the player and the GM to determine what its cost should be. All Slotted Abilities consume Endurance and/or AP, but determining exactly how much of those resources a Slotted Ability consumes is where much of the challenge of creating a Slotted Ability lies.

As mentioned before, the number of D6s of effectiveness an Ability has is a good starting point when determining its cost. However, there are many more factors that play into how costly an Ability is. A single Ability can have several Traits aside from D6s of effectiveness that must be considered when determining an Ability’s relative strength, and the appropriate cost to match. For example, an Ability that deals 3D6 damage to several characters should cost more than an Ability that deals the same amount of damage to a single character. Same goes for any other Trait an Ability may have.

Once you have a general idea of how powerful your character’s Ability is relative to the rest of the campaign by looking at its Trait and D6s of effectiveness, you now have to translate that into a concrete Endurance and AP cost. An average Ability consumes 2 AP and 2-4 Endurance. If your Ability isn’t remarkably weak or powerful, then it’s more than likely that you can assign a cost somewhere within this range and call it a day. But sometimes, an Ability will lean more towards either extreme, and so you’ll have to do some adjusting. While it’s encouraged to make most of your character’s Slotted Abilities balanced relative to each other, it’s completely acceptable to have a few that are considerably weaker or stronger, so long as it has an appropriate cost and doesn’t upset the balance of the campaign.

### Overcharged Abilities

Overcharged abilities are versions of your slotted abilities that cost additional Fate Points. Overcharges cannot be applied to Basic Actions or the Fatebreaker. The purpose of Overcharged abilities is to provide extra versatility to a slotted ability by giving it extra functions or potency with the additional Fate Point cost.

Preferably Overcharging an ability does not change its fundamental Activation Trait, an *Action* ability should stay as an *Action* ability.

Overcharging an ability can range from increasing its damage, its Save DC, its effective Range, and even giving it more status inflictions.

### Milestone Scaling

Upon reaching a Milestone, players may choose to go back to Abilities they’ve already made and tweak them in order to keep them up to speed with the rest of the campaign, with the GM’s help. This is to ensure that no characters fall behind the power curve that’s naturally present in all campaigns. Scaling an Ability upon reaching a Milestone can be as simple as adding on another D6 of effectiveness, or it can involve a much more significant overhaul that extends to the Ability’s other Traits. 

However, in special cases, you don’t need to wait until a Milestone before tweaking an Ability. If an Ability proves to be too strong or too weak, then the GM can step in and help the player rework it as soon as possible. Don’t be overly afraid to take risks and make mistakes, because you can always tweak your Abilities later. Just make sure that the core concept behind an Ability stays the same through reworks.

# Conflicts and Exploration

---

A GM declares whenever a conflict is about to begin. They will show the battle map to the players, and give them some time to prepare and come up with a strategy. Once everyone is ready, the conflict starts. 

Conflict is turn-based, meaning that both players and enemies will take turns acting. In the first round of most conflicts, the players act first, and can decide what order they take their actions in. After the first player has finished their turn, an opposing NPC acts next, and afterwards another player acts, and so on. Players and NPCs alternate turns until the end of the round.  Once every character in the conflict has completed their turns, the round is concluded and a new round begins. In the second round, the opposing NPCs act first, and in the third, the players act first, and so on.

## Space and Movement

Conflicts take place on a map divided into tiles. Movement, attacks, and many Abilities all use tiles to determine distance and area of effectiveness. All characters actively participating in a conflict must be present on the battlemap and occupy one or more tiles. 

Tiles can be obstructed by terrain and other characters. When making an attack or using an Ability, characters draw line of sight to their targets across the battlemap. If their line of sight is uninterrupted, then their target is valid, provided it’s within range of whatever action they’re attempting to take. If their line of sight is otherwise obstructed, then their target is invalid and cannot be directly targeted unless they move to a place where their line of sight is unobstructed.

A character is counted as adjacent to all other tiles both orthogonally and diagonally, and any characters that occupy tiles adjacent to another character become adjacent to them. 

A character may move a number of tiles on their turn equal to their Movement. All characters have a base 5 Movement, with an additional \+1 Movement per 2 points of Agility. Movement does not cost AP, and can be done at any point in the character’s turn. For instance, a character may move 3 tiles, make a Basic Attack, move 2 tiles, and then use an Ability. Characters cannot move through obstructions and spaces occupied by hostile characters. Characters may move through spaces occupied by allied characters.

### Difficult Terrain

Some areas on the battle map may be marked as Difficult Terrain. A character moving through Difficult Terrain must expend Two Movement per tile, instead of just one. Difficult Terrain may be natural to the environment, or may be created by certain Abilities.

### Dangerous Terrain

Some areas on the battle map may be marked as Dangerous Terrain. A character who moves through dangerous terrain automatically takes 1d6 damage, the type of which depending on the properties of the terrain. A character ending any of their subsequent turns in Dangerous Terrain after entering it for the first time takes the damage again until they move to normal terrain. The damage that dangerous terrain deals may vary depending on the situation, such as if it was created by another character’s Ability.

## HP & Evasion

Every character has a limited number of Hit Points (HP), starting at 20 and increasing by 5 per point of Vitality. A character’s HP is reset at the end of every conflict, and barring any extraneous circumstances, characters always start a conflict with full HP. 

Every character has an Evasion of 10 plus their Agility Attribute. To land a successful attack, the result of the attack roll must be greater than the target’s Evasion. A roll less than or equal to the target’s Evasion misses. If an attack lands, the target takes a certain amount of damage, typically determined by another dice roll, which is then subtracted from their HP. 

If a character is reduced to 0 HP, they take a Mortal Wound, and their HP is reset back to its maximum value. A character can take up to two Mortal Wounds, and if a character takes damage greater than their current HP and can take a Mortal Wound, the rest of that damage spills over to the HP they regain. If a character is reduced to 0 HP and cannot take any more Mortal Wounds, then they become Knocked Out and unable to fight. Their Evasion drops to 0, they automatically fail all saves, and all attacks against them are treated as critical hits.

A character who is Knocked Out cannot act, and must make a Death Save on their turn against a DC of 10, recording each success or failure. Upon reaching three successes, the character regains consciousness and is brought back to 1 HP. Upon reaching three failures, the character dies. Rolling a 20 on a Death Save counts as two successes, and rolling a 1 counts as two failures. A Death Save cannot be modified in any way. If a conflict ends with a character Knocked Out, that character regains consciousness and is brought back to full HP. 

A character who is Knocked Out may also be brought back to consciousness with a successful Heal check made by an adjacent character against a DC of 15\. Additionally, if a character who is Knocked Out regains HP through some external means, such as with an Ability, they immediately regain consciousness.

If a character who is Knocked out takes damage equal to their maximum HP before regaining consciousness, they immediately die regardless of any Death Saves.

### Mortal Wounds

When a character is reduced to 0 HP, they must roll a D20 to see what kind of Mortal Wound they incur. Mortal Wounds are persistent negative effects that stay with your character until they are cleared, typically with some kind of Ability, or until your character takes a Rest. They are distinct from Status Effects, and Abilities that clear or otherwise interact with Status Effects do not affect Mortal Wounds unless otherwise stated. Similarly, Abilities that interact with Mortal Wounds do not affect Status Effects unless otherwise stated. 

### Temporary HP

Temporary HP is a form of Health Points separate from your regular Health Points. It is usually gained through the use of certain abilities. When taking damage, Temporary HP is reduced before a character’s normal HP. You can only have one instance of Temporary HP at a time, with the strongest instance taking precedence. For example, if you have 10 Temporary HP, and you’re granted 15 Temporary HP, your Temporary HP is increased to 15, not 25\.

Temporary HP can come in many forms, such as a constructed bubble that surrounds your character, a sudden surge of vitality motivating your character, or simply a suit of armor they wear into battle. 

## Rest

When the GM deems it appropriate, a character can take a Rest, clearing all their Mortal Wounds and restoring all their Fate Points. However, the GM may choose to only restore FP or clear Mortal Wounds, not both. Abilities may be tied to a resource that is replenished only whenever a character Rests, or Abilities may only be used a certain number of times until a character takes a Rest.

## Action Points

Every character starts their turn with 3 Action Points (AP), which they may spend on any of the actions available to them. The cost of these actions vary, with more impactful actions, which are typically powerful Abilities, costing more AP to execute. Once a character no longer has any AP to spend, then their turn ends, however they may still use any unexpended movement if they wish. 

## Standard Combat Actions

Aside from their Abilities, a character may take one of many Standard Combat Actions on their turn. These actions are available to all characters.

### Basic Attack 

**1 AP**

Make a Basic Attack against a character within range. Subsequent Basic Attacks made after the first one on your turn cost 2 AP and do not benefit from modifiers.

### Dash

**2 AP**

Gain Movement equal to your maximum Movement value.

### Jump

**1 AP**

Jump a number of tiles equal to half your Agility Attribute rounded up either vertically or horizontally, to a minimum of 1 tile and a maximum of 6 tiles in either direction.

### Shove

**1 AP**

Make a melee attack roll with Martial against a target. On a success, you push them back a number of spaces equal to  half your Martial Attribute rounded up, to a minimum of 1 space and a maximum of 3 spaces. You cannot Shove characters larger than you.

### Grapple

**1 AP**

Make a melee attack roll with Martial against  a target. On a success, they become Grappled. A Grappled character is forced to move with the character grappling them, and all attacks against them are made with Advantage. A Grappled character may attempt to free themselves on their turn by performing a contested Martial or Agility check against the character grappling them, with each character choosing which Attribute to roll with. You cannot Grapple characters larger than you.

### Defend

*Reaction, 1/round*

Gain Resistance to an incoming attack. Because of the stress of bracing yourself, you only have 2 AP on your next turn instead of 3\.

### Hide

**1 AP**

A character not within line of sight of a hostile character may Hide on their turn with a successful Sneak check against a DC of 10\. Upon doing so, they become Hidden. Invisible characters can always Hide.

Attackers must roll a contested Spot Something check against the Hidden's Sneak before they are able to hit the Hidden target. If the enemy fails this roll, the Hidden target becomes an invalid target for any single target attack, and AoE attacks may only hit them if said AoE hits another player that isn’t the hidden, or if the purpose of the AoE is to reveal Hidden targets.

### Opportunity Attack

*Reaction, Armor-Breaking*

If an opposing character leaves your Melee range without disengaging, you are free to roll your Basic Attack against them once. 

### Disengage

**2 AP**

Disengaging allows a character to move without provoking Opportunity Attacks, although they will still be susceptible to traps and Dangerous Terrain.

### Interact

**1 AP**

Interact with an element of the environment, such as a lever or a door. The AP cost of interacting with an object may increase as per the GM’s discretion, particularly if the action involves a Skill check.

### Treat

**1 AP**

Spend another AP to remove an additional stack beyond the free status save at the start of your turn.

### Recover

**3 AP**

Immediately regain all Endurance. This action must be taken on the start of a character’s turn, and no other actions can be taken thereafter. Using Recover will also grant the user the option to clear 1 status effect they’re currently afflicted with.

### Save

**1 AP**

Make a Heal check against DC 15\. On success, Bring an adjacent Knocked Out character back to consciousness with 1 HP. 

## Endurance

More often than not, a character will have to expend some Endurance (END) in order to use one of their Abilities, corresponding to the END cost listed above the Ability’s description. This represents the toll that their Abilities demand from them, and may be associated with their stamina, mana, ammunition, or whatever the character needs to perform their Abilities. All characters start with a maximum of 10 END, which is noted on their character sheet, and begin all conflicts with all 10 END available for them to spend.

END can be recovered in a number of ways. All characters recover 1 END at the end of their turn, with an additional 1 END per 2 points of GRT. Characters always recover at least 1 END when regaining Endurance this way, even if their GRT is negative, unless they are affected by an Ability that prevents them from doing so. Additionally, all unspent AP is converted into END if a character decides to end their turn early, and characters may also choose to use their turn to Recover to regain all 10 of their END.
